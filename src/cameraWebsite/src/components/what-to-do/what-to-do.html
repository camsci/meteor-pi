How to use MeteorPi

Browsing our data online

To search for images collected by MeteorPi cameras, click on the "Search the Sky" tab at the top of this page.

You need to enter a range of dates to search between. To do this, click on the calendar icon next to the "From" and "To" boxes.

The "Min Duration" and "Max Duration" controls let you search for moving objects which were visible for short or long periods of time. For now, leave these settings untouched and click on "Search".

Is it a bird... is it a plane?

An example video of a meteor.

Not all the videos contain real moving objects. Sometimes the cameras trigger because of twinkling stars or video artifacts. Look through until you find a video that shows a moving object. The next challenge is to work out what it is. MeteorPi cameras pick up large numbers of satellites and planes, as well as meteors.

Meteors are distinctive, because they move much more quickly than planes and satellites, at speeds of over 10 kilometres each second. They are usually only visible for a fraction of a second as they burn up. To find more meteors, you may want change the "Max duration" field to one second and repeat your search.

Planes are often easy to spot because they have flashing lights. They are usually visible for 10-30 seconds, whereas meteors are gone in a fraction of a second. If you set the "Min duration" field to 10 or 20 seconds, most of the objects you'll see will be planes.

Satellites, like planes, are visible for much longer than meteors – at least 10-30 seconds. They move at a steady speed and they don't have flashing lights.

The flight paths of satellites are extremely predictable, and so if you think you've found one, you can try looking on a website like In-The-Sky.org to see if you can track down which satellite it was.

Alternatively, why not look up which satellites flew over the UK last night, and see if any MeteorPi cameras picked them up?
